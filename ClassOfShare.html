Classes Of Shares

There are different classes of Shares with a slight variable in classification. It is important to understand the difference between them, as it might present a direct effect on the total investment and its growth and value. All these different classes of shares are handled differently, especially the voting rights of each share class varies in nature and at the time of paying dividends.

The list of Classes of Shares mentioned below click any link.

    Class A Shares
    Class A shares refer to a classification of common stock that is accompanied by more voting right.

    Class A share are usually given to the company’s management team and thus have greater voting rights, ranging from five to ten votes per share.

    For example, a company allocate class A shares to its management giving them 5 to 10 times face value of class B shares. Companies classify stock for many reasons. In some cases, this is to grant excessive power to the company’s management to provide a suitable and strong defense against unforeseen events such as financial stress or economical setback. The purpose of doing this is to ensure that the founders and the management team keep control over the company’s board of directors and major corporate decisions. Class A shares would not be publicly traded, their availability is limited to company’s founders and higher management executives.

    Class A shares carry a front-end load.

    Definition of Front-End-Load:
    A front-end load is a commission or sales charge applied at the time of the initial purchase for an investment, usually with mutual funds and insurance policy purchases. It is deducted from the investment amount and, as a result, lowers the size of the investment. Front-end loads are paid to investment intermediaries, such as financial planners, brokers and investment advisors, as sales commissions as such, these sales charges are not part of a mutual fund’s operating expenses. There may be 12b-1 fees associated with Class A Share, these fee structure probably be lower than other class of shares such Class B and Class C, resulting a lower operating expense in this class. Buying Class, A shares is a good option to invest. Although the commission must be paid up front, since it is regarded as Front Load, but the investor could also get discounts on these purchases, in addition to the lower 12b-1 and other marketing and distribution fees.

    Class B shares have fewer voting rights in comparison to the Class A Shares. Further they carry a back-end load,
    A back-end load is a fee (sales charge or load) that investors pay when selling mutual funds shares, and the fee amounts to a percentage of the value of the share being sold. A back-end load can be a flat fee or can gradually decrease over time, usually within five to 10 years. In the latter case, the fee percentage is highest in the first year and decreases yearly until the specified holding period ends, at which time it drops to zero.

    The limit to hold on to Class B share is usually Six-year period. So, deciding to sell these shares before the typical six year period could incur exit charges, which would lower the profit on the original investment. Though these charges becomes less and less each year, but a charge has to paid if sold before the complete term of six years. These charges are referred as “Contingent Deferred Sales Charges or CDSC.

    Since there is no up-front cost to pay, all the invested monies goes into the fund itself. An added advantage is having better 12b-1 rate on this class of share with lower management fees. There are always added advantage to hold Class B share for a longer period.

    To have a better understanding of these charges a tool “analysis software” is available at www.Finra.org.

Class C shares are a type of mutual fund shares. The main difference between class C shares and the other two mutual fund shares is, that class C shares are level-load. This means the total amount of money paid to the mutual fund is invested in shares. No fees are normally charged at the front end. However, Class C shares tend to have higher operating expenses. In all cases, the load is paid to a financial intermediary and is not included in a fund’s operating expenses. – Class C shares are like Class A shares in all aspects, except that the Class C shares lack voting rights.

Class C shares have a lower “Contingent Deferred Sales Charges or CDSC, than Class B Shares. The requirement to hold Class C share is lower than six years. Class C share probably incur higher operating costs than Class A shares due to their increased 12b-1 fee structure. The management and operational cost for this class of share tend to rise overtime. It is a good class for a short-term investing since the sales charge may be minimal or nothing at all. The major downside is their higher annual expenses while you kept for a long period of time.

Each class of stock comes with its own package of features, such as voting rights, price, payout priority (dividends, etc.) thus associating several advantages and disadvantages with each share class.

It is important to always carefully check the details regarding a company’s stock classes to determine exactly what is being offered with each class.
